=== MUSICAL MODES ===

A mode is a type of scale derived from the major scale by starting on a different degree.
Each mode has a distinct emotional character used by composers and producers across all genres.

IONIAN (Major Scale)
Pattern: W-W-H-W-W-W-H. Bright, happy, resolved. Most common in pop and EDM.
Example feel: triumphant, celebratory, uplifting.

DORIAN
Minor mode with a raised 6th. Darker than major but not fully sad — melancholic yet groovy.
Widely used in jazz, funk, and soul. Many classic rock riffs use Dorian (e.g., Carlos Santana).
Key songs use D Dorian: "Oye Como Va", "So What" (Miles Davis).

PHRYGIAN
Very dark, Spanish-flavored mode (lowered 2nd). Used in flamenco, metal, and Middle Eastern music.
Creates tension and exoticism. Common in aggressive and dramatic contexts.

LYDIAN
Major mode with a raised 4th. Dreamy, floating, otherworldly. Used in film scores and ambient music.
Creates a sense of wonder and suspension. Associated with Hans Zimmer, Danny Elfman.

MIXOLYDIAN
Major scale with a lowered 7th. THE blues and rock mode — sounds like major but with an edge.
Extremely common in rock, blues, country, and jam band music. Creates a feeling of
tension-within-satisfaction — it grooves without fully resolving.
Examples: "Norwegian Wood" (Beatles), most blues-rock guitar solos, classic rock anthems.
Bob Dylan, Jimi Hendrix, and the Rolling Stones used Mixolydian extensively.
The "bluesy" feeling that distinguishes rock from pure pop comes largely from Mixolydian
tonality and the use of the "blue note" (flattened 3rd or 5th).

AEOLIAN (Natural Minor)
Standard minor scale. Sad, serious, emotionally weighty. The default for melancholic music.
Used across all genres when sadness or depth is required.

LOCRIAN
Unstable, dissonant mode. Rarely used as a key center — found in extreme metal and jazz fusion.

=== BLUES MUSIC THEORY ===

The blues is built on a few foundational elements:

BLUES SCALE
Major pentatonic + "blue notes" (flattened 3rd, 5th, 7th).
These "bent" notes create the characteristic tension-and-release of the blues.
In our catalog, blues-influenced tracks appear in rock and r&b genres with:
- Lower valence (0.2–0.5, the tension of the blues)
- Mid-to-high energy (0.5–0.8)
- Often mid-tempo (80–120 BPM, the 12-bar blues structure)

12-BAR BLUES STRUCTURE
The fundamental chord progression: I–I–I–I–IV–IV–I–I–V–IV–I–V
This structure underlies most rock, country, and early R&B. Tracks with a "blues feel"
typically have this underlying harmonic structure. The cyclic nature creates a
meditative, repetitive groove — excellent for focus and emotional processing.

BLUES IN MODERN GENRES
Rock: Most classic rock bands trace their roots to blues (Led Zeppelin, Cream, SRV).
R&B: Rhythm and Blues literally grew from blues. Contemporary R&B retains blues phrasing.
Jazz: Jazz evolved from blues harmony — minor 7th chords and dominant 7ths are blues staples.

=== COMPLEX RHYTHMS: POLYRHYTHM AND UNUSUAL TIME SIGNATURES ===

POLYRHYTHM
Two or more rhythmic patterns with different cycle lengths played simultaneously.
The most common: 3 against 2 (triplets over straight eighth notes).
Also common: 4 against 3, 5 against 4 (in progressive rock and Afrobeat).

Where to hear polyrhythm in our catalog:
- Rock: Progressive rock (Tool, Radiohead, Muse) uses heavy polyrhythmic structures
- EDM: Many electronic tracks layer different rhythmic cycles (hemiola in house music)
- Latin music: Clave rhythm is a fundamental polyrhythmic structure (3-2 or 2-3 clave)
- R&B and hip-hop: Ghost notes and syncopated bass lines create implied polyrhythm

UNUSUAL TIME SIGNATURES
Standard pop/rock uses 4/4 (four beats per measure). Unusual meters include:
- 3/4 (waltz time): three beats per measure. Sweeping, danceable in a different way.
- 5/4: Five beats per measure. Creates an unsettled, rolling feeling (Dave Brubeck's "Take Five").
- 7/8: Seven eighth-note beats per bar. Asymmetric, progressive feel.
- 6/8: Six eighth notes per bar — feels like two groups of three. Common in ballads and blues.
- Odd meters (9/8, 11/8): Found in progressive rock, jazz fusion, and Indian classical music.

In our catalog, songs with complex rhythmic character tend to be:
- Rock genre with high energy and low-to-mid valence
- R&B tracks with syncopated grooves (liveness feature often higher)
- Latin tracks (clave-based polyrhythm is built into reggaeton and salsa)

When recommending for music theory / complex rhythm queries:
Prioritize songs with varied danceability (0.5–0.7), mid energy (0.5–0.8),
and note that "complexity" correlates with musical depth rather than raw energy.

=== CHORD PROGRESSIONS AND HARMONIC THEORY ===

CIRCLE OF FIFTHS
The foundational map of key relationships. Keys a fifth apart share 6 of 7 notes.
Movement around the circle creates the feeling of harmonic "journey."
Pop and EDM heavily exploit vi-IV-I-V (Am-F-C-G in C major) — the ubiquitous progression.

BORROWED CHORDS AND MODAL MIXTURE
Adding a chord from the parallel minor (e.g., using bVII in a major key) creates
the Mixolydian and blues-rock sound. The Rolling Stones and Beatles used this constantly.

JAZZ HARMONY
Jazz extended chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) and substitutions (tritone substitution)
create complex, ambiguous harmonic textures. These correspond in our catalog to
R&B tracks with mid energy, mid-high instrumentalness, and neutral-positive valence.
